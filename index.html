What is an OTC Stock?

This guide provides an interactive way to understand Over-the-Counter (OTC) stocks. Think of it like this: major stock exchanges (like the NYSE) are like big, formal department stores with strict rules for the products they sell. OTC markets are more like a massive, decentralized network of specialty shops and markets, each with different standards. This application will help you navigate this world, from its structure to the risks and rewards involved.

Two Worlds of Trading

The biggest difference between OTC and regular stocks is *where* and *how* they are traded. Let's explore the key features to understand the fundamental differences in how they operate, which directly impacts transparency, cost, and risk.

      Who's in Charge?

      While the OTC market is "decentralized," it's not the Wild West. A multi-layered system of oversight exists to protect investors and maintain market integrity. This diagram shows the key players and their roles in regulating the brokers and dealers who operate within the OTC ecosystem.

      SEC

      The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission sets the rules. A recent rule change (15c2-11) indirectly forces transparency by restricting brokers from quoting prices for companies that don't provide public information.

      →

      FINRA

      The Financial Industry Regulatory Authority oversees broker-dealers. All brokers trading OTC stocks must be FINRA members and follow rules like "Best Execution" to get a fair price for their clients.

      →

      OTC Markets Group

      A private company that provides the trading platforms and organizes the market into the tiers (OTCQX, OTCQB, Pink) you explored earlier, creating a market-driven incentive for disclosure.

      Key Takeaways

      It's a Different Market: OTC is a decentralized network, not a centralized exchange. This is the source of its unique characteristics.
      Tiers Matter: The OTCQX, OTCQB, and Pink tiers indicate different levels of company disclosure and risk. They are your first guidepost for research.
      High Risk, High Potential: OTC stocks offer access to early-stage companies but require extra due diligence, caution, and specific trading strategies like using limit orders.
